01 — Cultivating a Fulfilling Sex Life

Sex Therapy & Intimacy

This practice is sex-positive, kink-aware, and poly-informed. Therapy here is a safe space to talk openly about intimacy, desire, and connection — without shame, and without being measured against a "normal" that was never built for everyone. Seeking help for your sex life can feel vulnerable; the aim is to normalise that, and to help you build the sex life you actually want.

A Sex-Positive Heritage

Pleasure, consent, and gender diversity are not new ideas

Long before colonial-era shame reshaped South Asian attitudes toward the body, Indian traditions treated physical pleasure as a healthy part of a whole life — and recognised a spectrum of genders and desires openly. Understanding this heritage is part of what makes this practice sex-positive and gender-affirming: these values are a return to something indigenous, not an import.

In both the West and modern India, the Kamasutra is often flattened into a mere "sex manual" or dismissed as obscene — a reading shaped by later moral frameworks that treat the body as ephemeral and physical pleasure as sinful, permissible only for reproduction.

Written by the sage Vatsyayana on the banks of the Ganges, the text actually presents physical pleasure as a positive, healthy aspect of living — something to be cultivated thoughtfully alongside one's spiritual and material goals, not in opposition to them.

Composed between roughly the 4th century BCE and the 3rd century CE, the work is not a single book but a collection of seven books — 36 chapters and some 1,250 verses. Only a fraction concerns sexual technique.

Book One frames a "good life" through the four Purusharthas — Dharma (moral values), Artha (material well-being), Kama (pleasure), and Moksha (liberation). Later books address emotional intimacy, the design of a home that nurtures joy, and social life. Book Six describes the Ganikas — not prostitutes in the modern sense, but accomplished, educated women of the cultured elite.

The Kamasutra explicitly rejects the idea that only a man's pleasure matters, or that a woman depends entirely on a man for her own satisfaction. Vatsyayana observes that a man's desire tends to ignite quickly like fire, while a woman's rises and settles more slowly, like water — a difference to be met with patience, not ignored.

Crucially, the text frames intimacy around active, ongoing consent: touch, closeness, and engagement are described as appropriate only when they are genuinely desired. It is a strikingly woman-centred ethic for its era.

Beyond the physical, Vatsyayana writes about love, emotional bonds, and how partners keep a relationship fresh over time. He even treats healthy lovers' quarrels as part of sustaining excitement — provided they rest on deep mutual trust.

The described dynamic of disagreement and reconciliation is notably woman-centred, emphasising humility and repair rather than dominance — a remarkably modern picture of intimacy as something actively tended.

Ancient texts — including the Susruta Samhita, the Narada Smriti, and the Kamasutra — recognised a range of sexualities and gender identities, often grouped under the idea of Tritiya Prakriti, a "third nature." Vedic literature describes multiple categories of gender-diverse and same-sex-attracted people, among both men and women.

Transgender and non-binary people — including communities today known as Hijras — lived openly, with socially recognised identities, long before the systemic erasure of later eras.

Much of the conservatism that surrounds sex and gender in South Asia today is not indigenous — it is inherited. British colonial rule imported Victorian morality and enshrined it in law.

Statutes such as the Criminal Tribes Act and penal codes targeting non-reproductive or gender-non-conforming expression criminalised and stigmatised ways of life that earlier Indian society had long accommodated, sharply narrowing the culture's inherited tolerance.

Temples at Khajuraho, Konark, and Kanchipuram carry intimate and same-sex imagery on their walls. Far from being read as obscene, these sculptures were understood as reflections of the mysteries of life, learning, and human nature.

Hindu mythology returns again and again to fluid gender: Ardhanarishvara (half-Shiva, half-Parvati), Krishna dressing as a woman in the Gita Govinda tradition, deities draped in the garments of another gender — all gesturing at identity as something fluid rather than fixed and binary.

Shared as cultural and historical context, not clinical or religious instruction. This heritage informs the affirming, non-judgmental lens of the practice — your own beliefs, background, and boundaries are always what guide the work in session.

How This Practice Works

Transparency, from intake to invoice

Being queer, trans, or kink-inclined is never the pathology.

The distress that brings people to therapy often comes from the outside — minority stress, systemic transphobia, purity culture, the daily weight of navigating an unaccommodating world. The work is to build resilience against those external pressures while celebrating who you actually are, not to "fix" your identity.

Names & Pronouns

Insurance may require a legal name on billing documents, but clinical notes and every internal communication always use your chosen name and pronouns. Systems are in place so you are never deadnamed in session or in the waiting room.

Inclusive Intake

Intake paperwork is designed inclusively — with space for your chosen name, pronouns, and a full spectrum of relationship structures, not just a box for "married" or "single."

Cultural Humility

As an allied clinician, Dr. Singh is committed to continuous consultation, education, and decentering personal assumptions — so the burden of "teaching your therapist" never falls on you.
